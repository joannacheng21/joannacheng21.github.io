Introduction/Background

We all depend on crops as a major part of our global food supply, and as a result, the swift detection of crop diseases is essential to maximizing the output of our fields and ensuring the health of the ingredients used in everyday products. However, manual detection of plant diseases is tedious; during the process of identification, disease continues to propagate rapidly. It also requires a high level of expertise and can be highly error prone [3]. Many of these reasons have spurred research efforts to use machine learning and deep learning to train models to identify diseased plants as well as the specific type of disease in order to aid in quick prevention of the spread of harmful blights that can ruin fields of crops. For example, Kurlkarni et al. predicted several types of diseases each for images of five species of crops using a random forest classifier and was able to achieve an average accuracy of 93% [4]. Other papers have ventured beyond traditional machine learning methods; one group used CNNs to classify a dataset of diseased and healthy plant leaves to achieve a 99.3% accuracy [1]. We plan to use the PlantVillage dataset, which contains 54,303 images of healthy and diseased plant leaves from 13 species, including apples, corn, grapes, and tomatoes, with various diseases such as black rot, common rust, and bacterial spot. It can be found here.

Problem Definition

The health of our crops is crucial for meeting our daily needs. Unfortunately, many people lack the knowledge to accurately identify diseases in their plants. To address this issue, we are working on developing a machine learning model that can distinguish between healthy and unhealthy leaf images across various plant species and diseases. Our model could have significant applications in the agriculture industry, as detecting diseased plants is essential for ensuring food security and protecting ecosystems from harm.

Preprocessing Methods

Contrast Enhancement

  • Utilize scikit-image library (the rescale_intensity method) to increase the contrast in the plant images, which would make it easier for our CNN model to identify the plants from the background.

Smoothing Edges

  • Utilize gaussian function within the skimage.filters module to smooth edges and remove noise from the leaf images. Using the gaussian function on the plant images would remove any unnecessary features that don't correlate with disease in the plant leaves, helping our algorithms perform better.

Grayscale

  • Utilize rgb2gray method from the skimage.color module to convert the colored plant leaf images to grayscale. Training our model with grayscale images would help eliminate color bias from the model and minimize the computational complexity of training.

Algorithms/Models

Convolutional Neural Network

  • Train a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) through TensorFlow by using Conv2D and MaxPooling2D layers. First, the image features will be extracted with convolutional layers. Then, with the FC layers and feature information, the model will conclude whether the leaf is from a healthy or unhealthy plant.

Decision Tree Classifier

  • Use DecisionTreeClassifier class from Scikit to classify leaf data into classes of being healthy or unhealthy. Scikit used the CART (Classification and Regression Trees) algorithm to back its decision tree classifier, which diverges from the C4.5 algorithm in that it doesn't compute rule sets.

Bagging Classifier

  • Use sklearn.ensemble.BaggingClassifier to classify our data. This classifier works by forming predictions based on randomized subsets of data. By combining the predictions, the classifier forms a final prediction. By randomizing, this classifier is useful since it reduces variance, helping reduce overfitting.

Quantitative Metrics

  • Accuracy: proportion of correctly identified diseased plants
  • Precision: True Positives/(True Positives + False Positives)
  • Recall: True Positives/(True Positives + False Negatives)
  • F1 Score: Harmonic mean of precision and recall

Project Goals/Expected Results

Our goal is to develop an effective model to identify diseased and healthy plants. We aim to achieve a classification accuracy, precision, recall rate, and F1 Score above 0.90.
